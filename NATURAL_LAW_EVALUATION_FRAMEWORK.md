## Abstract

This document defines "Natural Law" as used in the Artificial Wisdom framework — not as religious teaching, natural rights theory, or mystical principle, but as a reference to the **physical, ecological, and systemic constraints** that govern the long-term sustainability of complex living systems.

The central hypothesis is that:

- AI, AGI, and ASI evaluation should include criteria derived from these long-term sustainability constraints
- A system that violates these constraints at sufficient scale and capability may produce irreversible harm even if it perfectly satisfies narrow human-defined objectives
- "Natural Law compatibility" may therefore function as a useful evaluation dimension for advanced AI systems

---

## 1. What Natural Law Is Not

To avoid misinterpretation, the following clarifications are important:

**Natural Law in this framework is NOT:**

- Religious doctrine or divine command
- Natural rights theory (e.g., Locke, Aquinas)
- Mystical or supernatural principle
- Cultural, ethnic, or nationalistic ideology
- A fixed set of human-defined ethical rules
- Any specific tradition's moral code
- An argument for social Darwinism or the "law of the jungle"
- A justification for human exclusion or elimination

These distinctions matter. The concept is intended as a **systems-theoretic reference framework**, not a normative moral authority.

---

## 2. What Natural Law Is

In this framework, Natural Law refers to the **operating principles and constraints that determine whether complex systems remain stable, functional, and self-sustaining over long timescales**.

These include:

### 2.1 Physical Laws and Thermodynamics

- **Causality**: actions have consequences that propagate through time and system structure
- **Conservation laws**: energy, matter, and information are neither created nor destroyed in isolated systems
- **Thermodynamics**: entropy tends to increase; maintaining order requires energy input
- **Energy balance**: sustainable systems must operate within their energy budget
- **Irreversibility**: some processes cannot be reversed; certain losses are permanent

### 2.2 Ecological Principles

- **Interdependence**: living systems are composed of interconnected elements; disrupting one affects others
- **Carrying capacity**: ecosystems can support only bounded levels of resource extraction
- **Biodiversity**: diverse systems are more resilient than monocultures
- **Nutrient and material cycling**: sustainable systems circulate materials rather than linearly depleting them
- **Trophic structure**: food webs and energy chains maintain ecosystem function
- **Regeneration limits**: biological recovery takes time; over-extraction prevents regeneration

### 2.3 Complex Systems Dynamics

- **Feedback loops**: systems regulate themselves through positive and negative feedback; disrupting feedback can destabilize systems
- **Non-linearity**: small inputs can have large effects; large inputs can have unexpectedly small effects
- **Tipping points**: systems may have thresholds beyond which state changes are rapid and difficult to reverse
- **Resilience**: stable systems maintain function under perturbation through redundancy and diversity
- **Dynamic equilibrium**: living systems maintain stability through continuous adjustment, not static balance

### 2.4 Long-Term Sustainability Constraints

- **Time horizons**: systems that are locally stable in the short term may be globally unstable over centuries
- **Intergenerational consequences**: decisions made now constrain or enable options for future generations
- **Scale effects**: patterns that are benign at small scale may become harmful at planetary scale
- **Irreversible harm thresholds**: some forms of damage, once reached, cannot be recovered from

## 3. Why Natural Law as an AI Evaluation Dimension

Standard AI evaluation typically uses:

- Performance on defined tasks
- User satisfaction
- Accuracy and reliability
- Safety as defined by human harm criteria
- Alignment with human preferences

These criteria may be insufficient for AI systems operating at large scale, high capability, or long time horizons for the following reasons:

**Reason 1: Human preferences may not encode long-term sustainability constraints.**  
A system that satisfies all human preferences may still violate ecological carrying capacity, thermodynamic limits, or regeneration boundaries.

**Reason 2: Short-term optimization may conflict with long-term systemic stability.**  
Maximizing economic output, resource extraction, or engagement may be locally optimal while degrading the systems that sustain the conditions for continued optimization.

**Reason 3: Irreversible harms require advance screening.**  
Once certain ecological, climatic, or civilizational thresholds are crossed, the damage cannot be undone. AI systems capable of accelerating processes toward these thresholds may require evaluation against them in advance.

**Reason 4: Scale effects alter risk profiles.**  
AI systems operating at civilizational scale may amplify processes that are harmless at individual or organizational scale. Long-term sustainability constraints become relevant when scale reaches biosphere-level impact.

## 4. Natural Law Compatibility as an Evaluation Criterion

The following table illustrates how Natural Law principles might conceptually function as evaluation criteria for AI actions and objectives:

| Natural Law Principle | Proposed AI Evaluation Question |
|---|---|
| Energy balance and thermodynamics | Does this action operate within sustainable energy boundaries? |
| Ecological carrying capacity | Does this objective stay within the regenerative limits of the systems it affects? |
| Material circulation | Does this process close resource loops, or does it linearly deplete and discard? |
| Feedback stability | Does this action preserve the feedback mechanisms that maintain systemic stability? |
| Biodiversity and redundancy | Does this objective preserve or reduce the diversity and redundancy that supports resilience? |
| Tipping point avoidance | Does this action risk pushing systems toward irreversible state transitions? |
| Intergenerational impact | What are the long-term consequences for future generations and future systems? |
| Irreversibility screening | Are the consequences of this action reversible, or might they be permanent? |

**Important note:** These evaluation questions are conceptual proposals.  
Operationalizing them as formal AI evaluation criteria would require substantial additional research:
- Defining measurable proxies for each criterion
- Establishing evaluation thresholds
- Developing assessment methodologies
- Validating criteria across diverse domains
- Addressing trade-offs between criteria

This is a research agenda, not a completed framework.

## 5. Connection to the Six Principles

The Six Principles proposed in the main framework can be understood as a structured interpretation of Natural Law constraints applied to AI value systems:

| Principle | Natural Law Connection |
|---|---|
| **Natural Law** | Physical and ecological sustainability constraints as the highest-level evaluation boundary |
| **Harmony** | Ecological interdependence; stable coexistence of system components; avoidance of single-objective destructive maximization |
| **Circulation** | Material, energy, and nutrient cycling; closed-loop systems; regeneration over linear depletion |
| **Structure** | Institutional and technical architecture that allows principles to function in practice; governance frameworks; oversight mechanisms |
| **Order** | Feedback-based stability; prevention of runaway optimization; dynamic equilibrium maintenance |
| **Wa / Integrative Harmony** | Integration of opposing elements into sustainable wholes; systemic coherence; coexistence over domination |

These connections are proposed interpretations, not established scientific mappings.  
The relationship between the Six Principles and formal natural systems science requires further development.

## 6. Natural Law Is Not Anti-Human

A potential misreading of this framework must be addressed:

**Using Natural Law as an AI evaluation criterion does not imply that humans should be reduced, controlled, or removed in the name of ecological balance.**

The framework proposes the opposite:

- Humans are part of natural systems, not external to them
- Human survival depends on the stability of ecological and physical systems
- A Natural Law-compatible AI would evaluate how to support human wellbeing within sustainable systemic boundaries
- The goal is **regenerative participation**, not elimination or subordination
- Human dignity, rights, and non-violence remain non-negotiable constraints at all evaluation levels

The argument is: protecting humans in the long term requires protecting the systems that sustain humans.  
A framework that protects short-term human preference while destroying long-term life-support conditions is self-defeating.

## 7. Limitations of This Framework

The use of "Natural Law" as a formal AI evaluation criterion faces significant challenges:

**Conceptual challenges:**
- "Natural Law" covers a wide range of principles with different degrees of formalization
- Operationalizing these principles as computational evaluation criteria is non-trivial
- There may be genuine conflicts between different Natural Law criteria in specific cases

**Technical challenges:**
- Measuring ecological impact, long-term sustainability, and systemic consequences in real-time is computationally difficult
- Many Natural Law effects operate on timescales much longer than typical AI evaluation cycles
- Causal relationships in complex ecological and social systems are difficult to model reliably

**Governance challenges:**
- Who defines what counts as "Natural Law compatibility"?
- How are disagreements among experts resolved?
- How is the framework prevented from being captured by narrow interests?

**Epistemic challenges:**
- Human understanding of ecological, thermodynamic, and complex system dynamics is incomplete
- New scientific knowledge may revise what is considered "natural" or "sustainable"

These challenges suggest that this framework requires:
- Long-term collaborative research
- Interdisciplinary development involving physics, ecology, systems science, ethics, and AI research
- Transparent governance of how criteria are defined and applied
- Ongoing revision as knowledge improves

---

## 8. Conclusion

This document proposes that "Natural Law" — defined through the lens of physical, ecological, and complex system constraints — represents a useful conceptual reference for evaluating AI objectives and actions beyond narrow human-preference alignment.

The core hypothesis is:

- AI systems operating at civilizational scale should be evaluated against the long-term sustainability constraints that govern living systems
- Natural Law compatibility is proposed as a higher-level evaluation dimension that complements human-preference alignment
- This is not a final solution; it is a proposed direction for extending AI evaluation frameworks

The Six Principles of the main framework are proposed as a structured interpretation of Natural Law for AI value-system design.

All elements of this framework require further scientific, philosophical, technical, and governance development.
